## Three Discoveries

### Discovery 1: Model Optimization Reduced Cost by 17% with Minimal Token Reduction

The shift from baseline agents to model-optimized agents produced a **17% cost reduction ($327.84 → $274.48)** while only reducing token count by **9% (430.6M → 394.3M)**.

The key insight: **not all tokens cost the same**. By shifting 100M tokens from Sonnet ($277 → $218) to Haiku ($5 → $19), the net cost reduction was $53.

This proves that model-tiering strategy works in practice, not just theory. The quality of output remained consistent while costs dropped significantly.

### Discovery 2: Haiku/Sonnet Ratio Shift Demonstrates Agent Tier Optimization Working

Week 1 had Haiku at **2x Opus usage**. Week 2 increased this to **6.5x Opus usage**.

This 3.25x increase in Haiku relative usage shows that the agent tier assignments are working:

- Simple tasks (documentation, scaffolding, repetitive operations) → Haiku
- Complex reasoning (architecture, design decisions, debugging) → Sonnet
- Strategic planning (cross-component coordination) → Opus

The model selection isn't just random—it's responding to the actual complexity distribution of tasks.

### Discovery 3: Type of Work Significantly Impacts Metrics

Week 1 was **planning-heavy** (1,553 issues created, 212K lines added). Week 2 was **infrastructure-heavy** (398 issues closed, 82K lines added).

This explains the apparent slowdown: closing issues and building infrastructure requires less code generation but more integration work, testing, and coordination.

The 4-day limit in Week 2 (vs 5-day in Week 1) despite lower costs suggests **higher throughput per dollar**—the system is doing more useful work, not just generating more tokens.

## Reflections

This analysis taught me important lessons about measuring agentic systems:

1. **Cost reduction ≠ token reduction** - Model tiering can reduce costs significantly with minimal token reduction by shifting to cheaper models.
1. **Ratios matter more than absolutes** - The Haiku-to-Opus jump from 2x to 6.5x is more meaningful than the absolute token count.
